# Lesson themes: emu-de-en-a1-01

> Applied CEFR A1 descriptors to evaluate vocabulary level-appropriateness and communicative function classification. Themes were identified by topic shifts in the conversation, and vocabulary was split into active (student-produced) and passive (teacher-introduced, student understood). Communicative functions follow the CEFR functional categories: describing, narrating, explaining, requesting information. The pedagogical arc shows a well-structured lesson moving from activation to input to controlled practice to freer practice, consistent with a PPP (Presentation-Practice-Production) approach.

## Lesson arc

The lesson follows a classic PPP structure. It opens with a warm-up conversation about Lukas's work week (activation of prior knowledge, surfacing of errors for the lesson topic). The teacher uses Lukas's natural error ("I go with the car") as a bridge into the planned topic: transport and directions. The presentation phase introduces transport vocabulary, verb collocations (take/drive/ride/walk), and the "go by" structure. Controlled practice follows with tense drills (present/past/future transport sentences). The lesson then transitions to directions vocabulary (position words, landmarks) and progresses through increasingly free practice: repeating directions, giving directions from a map, and finally a phone role-play giving directions to a colleague. The review phase consolidates with a correct/incorrect exercise and a summary of key error patterns. Homework is assigned for continued practice.

## Themes

### 1. Work and daily routine [00:00-02:32]

**Function:** narrating, describing | **Initiated by:** teacher (warm-up question) -> student-driven content

The opening warm-up covered Lukas's work situation: a new engineering project designing water supply systems for a 12-floor office building in Munich. This naturally led to discussing commuting, which the teacher used as a bridge to the lesson topic.

**Pedagogical intent:** Activate prior knowledge and personal context. Surface errors organically to motivate the lesson topic. The teacher skillfully used Lukas's "I go with the car" error to introduce the transport theme.

### 2. Transport vocabulary and verb collocations [02:32-11:30]

**Function:** describing, explaining | **Initiated by:** teacher (planned topic)

Core vocabulary introduction: eight transport types (bus, train, tram, underground, taxi, car, bicycle, on foot) with their verb collocations (take, drive, ride, walk, go by). Practice included present tense routines, past tense events, and future plans. The teacher introduced the by/with distinction and the "on foot" exception.

**Pedagogical intent:** Build a systematic vocabulary set with correct collocations. Address the L1 transfer error (mit -> with) explicitly. Drill tense usage across past/present/future time expressions.

### 4. Asking for and giving directions - map exercise [18:14-43:50]

**Function:** requesting information, explaining, narrating | **Initiated by:** teacher (planned activity) with student-initiated sub-topics

Extended practice phase using a city map. Progressed from teacher-gives/student-repeats to student-gives/teacher-follows, then to role-plays (tourist/local). Included asking for directions, giving multi-step directions, using landmarks, and handling communication breakdown ("Could you repeat that?"). Word order errors emerged under production pressure, with increasing self-correction over time.

**Pedagogical intent:** Move from controlled to freer practice. Build real-world communicative competence. The map provides visual scaffolding. Role-plays add social context (politeness phrases, clarification strategies). The progression tests whether vocabulary is moving from recognition to production.

### 5. False friends and L1 awareness [24:26-25:29]

**Function:** explaining, metalinguistic discussion | **Initiated by:** student (organic error)

A focused mini-segment on the become/bekommen false friend, triggered by Lukas's natural error. The teacher explained the mapping clearly and Lukas demonstrated understanding. This led to a brief metalinguistic discussion about L1 interference.

**Pedagogical intent:** Build metalinguistic awareness. Help the student recognize L1 transfer patterns so they can self-monitor. The become/get distinction is a high-frequency false friend for German learners.

#### Active vocabulary
| Term | Context | Level |
|------|---------|-------|
| get (nervous) | corrected from "become nervous" | A1 |

#### Passive vocabulary
| Term | Context | Level |
|------|---------|-------|
| false friend | teacher used the term | metalinguistic |
| transfer error | teacher used the term | metalinguistic |

#### Chunks
- "I get nervous" (vs "I become nervous")

#### Representative exchange
> **Lukas:** "I become nervous when I am lost in a new city."
> **James:** "Ah, careful with that one. You said 'I become nervous'. In English, 'become' means to change into something. Here, you want to say 'I get nervous'."
> **Lukas:** "Get nervous? But in German, 'bekommen' is 'to get'. Not 'to become'. This is very confusing."

### 6. Review and consolidation [44:50-55:05]

**Function:** explaining, self-assessment | **Initiated by:** teacher (planned review)

Systematic review covering: transport vocabulary recall (all 8 types), verb collocations, correct/incorrect sentence judgment (7 items covering all major error patterns), and a final free production task (phone directions to office). Homework assigned: write 3 sets of directions, practice W pronunciation, complete map worksheet.

**Pedagogical intent:** Test retention, reinforce error awareness through judgment tasks, and provide a final unscaffolded production opportunity. The correct/incorrect exercise is particularly effective because it forces explicit knowledge of the rules practiced.
