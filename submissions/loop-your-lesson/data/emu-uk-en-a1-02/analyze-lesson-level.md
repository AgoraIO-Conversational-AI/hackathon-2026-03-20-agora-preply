## Gaps

### Article system (a/an/the) (Accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Systematic omission of both definite and indefinite articles. Occurs in virtually every extended utterance. L1 transfer - Ukrainian has no articles. This is expected at A1 for Ukrainian speakers but is the highest-frequency error and must be a persistent focus. Evidence: #1, #2, #10, #11, #18, #19 and many more uncounted instances.

### Third person -s (Accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Omits -s/-es on third person singular present simple verbs. Occurs 7+ times explicitly, with many self-corrections. The rule is being acquired (self-correction shows awareness) but is not yet automatic. This is the core grammar target of the lesson and the most important gap to practice. Evidence: #3, #8, #10, #13, #14, #17.

### Preposition for time: at vs on (Accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Consistently uses "on" instead of "at" for clock times. L1 transfer from Ukrainian "о". Occurred twice and was explicitly addressed. The student can articulate the rule when prompted but does not apply it automatically. Evidence: #6, #9.

### Preposition for transport: by (Accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Used "on car" instead of "by car". Single instance but reflects a systematic gap in transport preposition collocations. Evidence: #16.

### Past simple tense (Accuracy) [exercise_focus: false]
Used present "wake" instead of past "woke" twice when describing past events. Not a lesson focus but relevant for future lessons. Evidence: #5, #7.

### Present simple vs present continuous (Accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Used present continuous ("I am working", "I am sitting", "I am meeting") for habitual actions that require present simple. Evidence: #20, #21.

### Missing subject "it" (Accuracy) [exercise_focus: false]
Omits subject "it" in copular constructions ("is bad", "is difficult"). Ukrainian pro-drop transfer. Evidence: #2, #22.

## Suggestions

### For teacher
1. **[P1]** Continue drilling third person -s through varied contexts - the self-correction behavior shows the rule is at the tipping point of acquisition. Use elicitation ("She...?") rather than explicit correction to strengthen self-monitoring.
2. **[P1]** Introduce article practice as a recurring micro-activity. Start with "a" for first-mention countable nouns in daily contexts ("a sandwich", "a project", "a clock"). Don't overwhelm with the/a distinction yet.
3. **[P2]** Reinforce preposition collocations through formulaic chunks: "at + time", "on + day", "in + month/season", "by + transport". Drill as fixed phrases rather than rules.
4. **[P2]** Address present simple vs continuous explicitly in the next lesson with clear contrast pairs: "I work every day" (simple) vs "I am working right now" (continuous).
5. **[P3]** Introduce past simple systematically in a future lesson (2-3 lessons ahead). The student has some awareness (woke) but cannot produce it reliably.

### For student
1. **[P1]** Practice describing your wife's or a friend's daily routine out loud every day. Focus on adding -s to every verb: "she wakes, she goes, she cooks, she reads". This is your most important practice activity.
2. **[P1]** When you say a noun, always ask yourself: does it need "a" or "the"? Start with "a" for things you mention for the first time: "a sandwich", "a project", "a meeting".
3. **[P2]** Practice saying clock times in English whenever you check the time. Use "half past", "quarter past", "quarter to" - not just the numbers.
4. **[P2]** Remember: "at" for times (at seven o'clock), "on" for days (on Monday), "by" for transport (by car). Make a note on your phone with these three rules.
5. **[P3]** Keep reading tech news in English. You're building vocabulary from context, which is exactly the right approach at this level.
