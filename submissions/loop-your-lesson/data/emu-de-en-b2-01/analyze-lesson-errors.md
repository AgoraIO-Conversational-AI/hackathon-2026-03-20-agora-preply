# Error analysis: emu-de-en-b2-01

> Applied the error taxonomy (grammar, vocabulary, pragmatic categories) with severity calibrated against CEFR B2 descriptors. German-English interlanguage patterns were the primary analytical lens, since the majority of errors trace to L1 transfer (false friends, preposition mapping, compound noun rules, relative pronoun system). At B2+, grammar is largely acquired; the error profile is characteristic of a learner transitioning to C1, where collocations, pragmatics, and prepositional idioms become the frontier.

## Errors

### #1 grammar/false_friend (moderate) [03:33]

**Original:** "I become very frustrated"
**Corrected:** "I get very frustrated"

German "bekommen" means "to receive", but its phonetic similarity to English "become" causes persistent substitution. At B2, this false friend should be known declaratively but still surfaces under cognitive load.

- **Communicative impact:** medium - could confuse a listener ("become frustrated" exists but sounds unnatural for habitual/emotional states)
- **L1 transfer:** Direct transfer from German "werden" / "bekommen" confusion. "Ich werde frustriert" uses "werden" (to become), reinforcing the false mapping.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher flagged the error directly at [03:42], student self-corrected at [03:50]
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #2 vocabulary/false_friend (moderate) [03:33]

**Original:** "the actual situation"
**Corrected:** "the current situation"

German "aktuell" means "current/present", not "actual". One of the most common German-English false friends.

- **Communicative impact:** medium - "actual situation" exists in English but means something different (the real/true situation vs. the present situation)
- **L1 transfer:** Direct lexical transfer from German "aktuell" -> English "actual". The student recognized it immediately when prompted.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher pointed it out at [04:06], student acknowledged at [04:13]
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #3 vocabulary/false_friend (moderate) [09:05]

**Original:** "We must be consequent about reducing production"
**Corrected:** "We must be consistent about reducing production"

German "konsequent" means consistent/rigorous, not "consequent" (which means "following as a result" in English).

- **Communicative impact:** medium - "consequent" exists in English but has a different meaning, causing genuine misunderstanding
- **L1 transfer:** Direct lexical transfer from German "konsequent". The semantic fields overlap slightly but the primary meanings diverge.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher corrected at [09:14], student repeated and noted it at [09:22]
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #4 grammar/noun_adjective_form (minor) [05:07]

**Original:** "environment protection"
**Corrected:** "environmental protection"

German compounds nouns freely (Umweltschutz = Umwelt + Schutz). English requires the adjective form for modification.

- **Communicative impact:** low - meaning is clear despite the error
- **L1 transfer:** German compound noun transfer. In German, "Umweltschutz" is a single compound; the student decomposed it but used the noun form instead of the adjective.
- **Correction strategy:** elicitation - teacher asked "how would we say that naturally?" at [05:30], student self-corrected at [05:36]
- **Exercise priority:** 3

### #6 grammar/relative_pronoun (moderate) [06:40]

**Original:** "the species which cannot adapt"
**Corrected:** "the species that cannot adapt"

Using "which" for animate referents. English distinguishes "who/that" (people/animals) from "which" (things).

- **Communicative impact:** low - grammatically noticeable but meaning preserved
- **L1 transfer:** German uses "die/der/das" for all relative clauses regardless of animacy. The student has no L1 cue for the who/which distinction.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher explained the rule at [06:48]
- **Exercise priority:** 3

- **Communicative impact:** low - understood but marked
- **L1 transfer:** Same as #6. German relative pronoun system does not encode animacy.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher corrected at [13:39]

### #8 grammar/relative_pronoun (minor) [50:44]

**Original:** "the documentary what I watched"
**Corrected:** "the documentary that I watched"

Using "what" as a relative pronoun. "What" is not a relative pronoun in standard English.

- **Communicative impact:** low - understood in context
- **L1 transfer:** German "was" can function as a relative pronoun (das, was ich gesehen habe), and its phonetic similarity to "what" triggers the transfer.
- **Correction strategy:** self_correction - student caught it immediately
- **Exercise priority:** 2

### #9 grammar/preposition (moderate) [10:02]

**Original:** "I am interested for this argument"

### #11 vocabulary/collocation (moderate) [15:18]

**Original:** "There was heavy evidence"
**Corrected:** "There was strong evidence"

Wrong adjective-noun collocation. English collocates "strong/compelling/overwhelming" with "evidence", not "heavy".

- **Communicative impact:** low - meaning clear but sounds non-native
- **L1 transfer:** German "schwer" (heavy) and "stark" (strong) have overlapping usage. "Schwerwiegende Beweise" (weighty evidence) may reinforce the "heavy" mapping.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher corrected at [15:30] and explained collocation pattern
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #12 pragmatic/directness (moderate) [10:50]

**Original:** "This argument is wrong."
**Corrected:** "I'd question that assumption" / "I'm not sure I agree with that premise"

Excessive directness in expressing disagreement, inappropriate for English academic/professional discourse.

- **Communicative impact:** high - could damage interpersonal relationships and be perceived as aggressive in English-speaking contexts
- **L1 transfer:** German academic discourse norms favor direct disagreement ("Dieses Argument ist falsch, weil..."). The student confirmed this cultural pattern at [11:14] and [27:54].
- **Correction strategy:** elicitation - teacher prompted rephrasing at [10:54], then taught hedging strategies extensively
- **Exercise priority:** 5

### #13 pragmatic/register (minor) [23:27]

Overly formal register for conversational context. The student defaults to formal/academic English in casual settings.

- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher noted the register mismatch at [23:32]
- **Exercise priority:** 3

### #14 grammar/noun_adjective_form (minor) [06:56]
