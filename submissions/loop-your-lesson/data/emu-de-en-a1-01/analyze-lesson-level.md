## Strengths

### Vocabulary acquisition speed (range)
Lukas acquires new vocabulary quickly within the lesson. Words introduced by the teacher (design, pharmacy, timetable, garage, landmark) are used correctly within minutes. He makes effective use of L1 cognates (taxi, zebra crossing) and cross-linguistic comparisons to anchor new knowledge.

### Metalinguistic awareness (interaction)
Lukas frequently makes explicit comparisons between German and English ("In German we say 'Gestern fuhr ich zur Arbeit' - the verb comes second"), asks why-questions about rules ("Why is foot different?"), and identifies L1 transfer patterns ("because in German we say 'mit dem Bus'"). This awareness is a strong predictor of progress.

### Self-correction ability (fluency)
By mid-lesson, Lukas begins catching V2 word order errors before completing the sentence (at 27:56, 33:19, 43:12). This indicates the rule is moving from explicit knowledge to monitoring, which is the critical step before automatization.

### Pragmatic competence (interaction)
Uses polite request forms correctly ("Excuse me, where is...?", "Sorry, could you repeat that?", "Could you help me?"). Maintains appropriate register throughout. Produces "You're welcome" naturally. These formulaic phrases are well-internalized.

### Task completion (coherence)
Completed all lesson tasks successfully: vocabulary listing, tense drill, direction repetition, map exercise, role-play, correct/incorrect judgment, and phone directions. The final phone task (48:12-49:16) was the most demanding and produced near-error-free output.

## Gaps

### V2 word order transfer (accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Most persistent error pattern. When a time expression or adverbial fronts the sentence, Lukas places the verb before the subject (German V2 rule). Occurred at least 4 times with additional self-corrections. This error impedes fluency and marks speech as non-native. At A1, some V2 transfer is expected for German speakers, but the frequency suggests it needs targeted practice.

### 3rd person singular -s (accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Three instances of omitted -s (she take, he drive, the bus go). This is an A1-level structure that should be emerging reliably. The student knows the rule (identifies it in the review exercise) but fails to apply it in spontaneous production. High exercise priority because it is frequent and visible.

### Past tense with time expressions (accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Three instances of present tense used with past time expressions (last Monday I take, last Friday I go, last week I go). The student can produce correct past forms when prompted (took, went, drove) but does not trigger them automatically from the time expression. This gap straddles A1/A2 boundary.

### Preposition collocations for transport (accuracy) [exercise_focus: true]
Used "with" instead of "by" twice despite correction. Deep L1 transfer (German "mit"). Needs repeated exposure and practice to override the L1 pattern.

### W/V pronunciation (pronunciation) [exercise_focus: false]
Consistent /v/ for /w/ substitution in W-initial words. Universal for German speakers. Not exercise-focus because it requires articulatory practice (mirror work, minimal pairs) rather than written exercises, and the teacher assigned specific pronunciation homework.

### become/get false friend (vocabulary) [exercise_focus: true]
Single instance but high communicative impact. Corrected within the lesson and student demonstrated understanding. Needs reinforcement to prevent reversion.

### Modal verb frame (accuracy) [exercise_focus: false]
One clear instance ("I can the street not find") plus the early "I must a new system" construction. German modal verb frame places the infinitive at the end of the clause. At A1, this is expected for German speakers and was addressed in the lesson. Lower priority than V2 and 3rd person -s.

## Suggestions

### For teacher
1. **[P1]** Focus next 2-3 lessons on SVO word order with fronted adverbials. Create sentence-building drills: "Yesterday + I + past verb + object." Use the student's engineering mindset - present it as a formula.
2. **[P1]** Drill 3rd person -s in varied contexts. Move beyond isolated sentences to short descriptions of routines ("My wife takes..., my colleague drives..., the bus goes...").
3. **[P2]** Introduce simple past irregular verbs systematically. Group by pattern (take/took, go/went, drive/drove, come/came). Use the transport context as the vehicle.
4. **[P2]** Revisit by/with/on preposition rule with new contexts beyond transport (by email, by phone, by hand).
5. **[P3]** Begin introducing "used to" and "usually" for habitual past vs present contrast - this builds toward A2 narration.

### For student
1. **[P1]** Practice word order rule daily: when you start a sentence with a time word (yesterday, last week, tomorrow), always say the subject next, then the verb. Write 5 sentences each day following this pattern.
2. **[P1]** When describing someone else's routine, always check: did I add -s? He takes, she drives, it goes. Say three "he/she/it" sentences about your colleagues each morning.
3. **[P2]** Make flashcards for irregular past tenses learned: take/took, go/went, drive/drove, break/broke. Test yourself with time expressions: "Last Monday I ___."
4. **[P2]** Remember: "by" for transport (by bus, by car), "with" for people (with my friend), "on" for foot (on foot). Write this rule on a sticky note at your desk.
5. **[P3]** Practice the W sound daily with the 10 words: where, what, when, why, want, walk, work, water, weather, wait. Round your lips before saying each word.
