## Errors

### #1 grammar/quantifier (moderate) [00:20]

**Original:** "I have very much work this week."
**Corrected:** "I had a lot of work this week."

The quantifier "very much" is used as a determiner before an uncountable noun. English requires "a lot of" or "much" (in negative/question contexts). Additionally, "have" should be "had" given the past time reference implied by "this week" in past context, though present tense is arguably acceptable for ongoing situations.

- **Communicative impact:** low - meaning is clear
- **L1 transfer:** Partial. German "sehr viel Arbeit" maps to "very much work" but English requires "a lot of work" in affirmative statements.
- **Correction strategy:** recast - teacher rephrased as "a lot of work"
- **Exercise priority:** 3

### #2 grammar/verb_tense (major) [00:35]

**Original:** "I must a new system... entwerfen?"
**Corrected:** "I must design a new system."

Missing main verb (vocabulary gap for "design") but also demonstrates German modal verb frame where the infinitive goes to the end of the clause. Student placed the object before finding the verb.

- **Communicative impact:** medium - communication was interrupted, required teacher assistance
- **L1 transfer:** Yes. German modal verb frame: "Ich muss ein neues System entwerfen" - infinitive at end. Student replicated this frame in English.
- **Correction strategy:** recast - teacher supplied the missing word "design"
- **Exercise priority:** 2

### #3 grammar/preposition (major) [01:45]

**Original:** "I go with the car."
**Corrected:** "I go by car." / "I drive."

Used "with" instead of "by" for transport. This is a direct transfer from German "mit dem Auto".

- **Communicative impact:** low - meaning is clear but sounds non-native
- **L1 transfer:** Yes. German "mit + transport" directly transferred. "Ich fahre mit dem Auto" -> "I go with the car." Classic German-English transfer error.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher flagged the error directly and explained the by/with rule
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #6 grammar/verb_tense (major) [07:31]

### #7 grammar/word_order (major) [08:21]

**Original:** "Yesterday drove I to work."
**Corrected:** "Yesterday I drove to work."

German V2 (verb-second) word order transferred to English. In German, when a time adverbial fronts the sentence, the finite verb stays second and the subject moves after it: "Gestern fuhr ich." In English, subject-verb order is fixed.

- **Communicative impact:** medium - requires listener effort to parse
- **L1 transfer:** Yes. Classic V2 transfer. German "Gestern fuhr ich zur Arbeit" has verb in position 2, pushing subject to position 3. This is the most characteristic German-English transfer error at this level.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher explained the rule and contrasted with German
- **Exercise priority:** 5

### #12 grammar/verb_complement (moderate) [13:14]

**Original:** "People explain me how to find the building."
**Corrected:** "People explain to me how to find the building."

Missing "to" in the indirect object construction with "explain". English requires "explain to someone", not "explain someone".

- **Communicative impact:** low - meaning clear
- **L1 transfer:** Yes. German "erklaeren + dative" does not require a preposition: "Leute erklaeren mir" -> "People explain me." The dative case handles what English does with "to".
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher inserted "to me"
- **Exercise priority:** 3

### #13 vocabulary/false_friend (major) [24:26]

**Original:** "I become nervous when I am lost in a new city."
**Corrected:** "I get nervous when I am lost in a new city."

Used "become" as a translation of German "bekommen" (which means "to get/receive"). English "become" means "werden" (to change into). This is one of the most well-known German-English false friends.

- **Communicative impact:** medium - "become nervous" is actually grammatical English but means a permanent transformation, not the intended temporary state
- **L1 transfer:** Yes. Classic false friend. German "bekommen" = English "get/receive". English "become" = German "werden". Student confused the pair.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher explained the false friend in detail
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #14 pronunciation/consonant (moderate) [19:48, 30:21, 37:43]

**Original:** "vhere", "vant"
**Corrected:** "where", "want"

Substituted /v/ for /w/ in W-initial words. German W is pronounced /v/, and the /w/ phoneme does not exist in German.

- **Communicative impact:** low-medium - recognizable to native speakers but can cause confusion (where/very, wine/vine)
- **L1 transfer:** Yes. German has no /w/ phoneme. German W = /v/. This is a universal German-English transfer pattern.
- **Correction strategy:** explicit - teacher corrected multiple times with articulation guidance ("round your lips")
- **Exercise priority:** 3
