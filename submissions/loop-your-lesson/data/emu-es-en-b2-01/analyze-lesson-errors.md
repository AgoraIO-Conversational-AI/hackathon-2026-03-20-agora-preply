# Error analysis: emu-es-en-b2-01

> Applied the error taxonomy (grammar, vocabulary, pragmatic) with severity calibrated against CEFR B2 descriptors. At B2, articles/basic prepositions should be acquired; errors in these areas are moderate-to-major. False friends and null subject omission are expected L1 transfer patterns for Spanish-English (Swan & Smith 2001, Odlin 1989) but at B2 they should be decreasing. Used interlanguage theory to identify fossilized L1 transfer patterns - particularly null subjects (pro-drop), prepositional calques from Spanish "de", and false cognates. Severity is raised for errors that are recurring and expected to be acquired at B2.

## Talk ratio

Student: 50% | Teacher: 50%

## Errors

### #1 grammar/verb_tense (minor) [01:10]

**Original:** "We had to make a meeting with the regional directors"
**Corrected:** "We had to hold a meeting with the regional directors"

Collocation error rather than pure tense error - "make a meeting" is a calque from Spanish "hacer una reunion". The verb tense itself is correct.

- **Communicative impact:** low - meaning is clear
- **L1 transfer:** yes - Spanish "hacer una reunion" directly translated as "make a meeting"; English uses "hold/have a meeting"
- **Correction strategy:** elicitation [18:43] - teacher later prompted student to identify correct collocation; student produced "hold a meeting" and "have a meeting"
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #2 vocabulary/false_friend (major) [01:24]

**Original:** "I had to assist to many meetings"
**Corrected:** "I had to attend many meetings"

Classic Spanish-English false friend: "asistir" = attend, not assist.

- **Communicative impact:** high - "assist" means "help", changing the meaning entirely
- **L1 transfer:** yes - "asistir a una reunion" (Spanish) = attend a meeting; "assist" (English) = help/aid
- **Correction strategy:** recast [01:31] - teacher rephrased with "attend"; student recognized the error and noted it is recurring
- **Exercise priority:** 5

### #3 vocabulary/false_friend (minor) [02:16]

**Original:** "It went quite well actually"
**Corrected:** "It went quite well, in fact"

Student initially used "actually" in the Spanish sense of "actualmente" (currently), then self-corrected to "in fact".

- **Communicative impact:** low - self-corrected quickly; the English "actually" can work in some contexts
- **L1 transfer:** yes - "actualmente" (Spanish) = currently; "actually" (English) = in fact/really
- **Correction strategy:** self_correction - student caught it and said "I mean, in fact"
- **Exercise priority:** 2

### #4 grammar/null_subject (major) [03:08]

**Original:** "many people think is not enough flexibility"
**Corrected:** "many people think it is not enough flexibility"

Missing expletive subject "it" in embedded clause.

- **Communicative impact:** medium - meaning recoverable but sounds markedly non-native
- **L1 transfer:** yes - Spanish is a pro-drop language; "piensan que no es suficiente" omits the subject naturally
- **Correction strategy:** explicit [03:16] - teacher pointed out the missing "it" and explained the embedded clause rule
- **Exercise priority:** 5

### #5 grammar/l1_structure_transfer (major) [04:05]

**Original:** "I am agree with the hybrid model"
**Corrected:** "I agree with the hybrid model"

Insertion of copula "am" from Spanish structure "estoy de acuerdo".

- **Communicative impact:** medium - meaning clear but a persistent B2 error suggesting fossilization
- **L1 transfer:** yes - "estoy de acuerdo" literally = "I am in agreement"; student adds "am" before "agree"
- **Correction strategy:** explicit [04:11] - teacher explained that "agree" is a verb, not an adjective, so no "to be" needed
- **Exercise priority:** 4

### #11 vocabulary/false_friend (major) [10:07]

**Original:** "the results were very sensible to how you measure them"
**Corrected:** "the results were very sensitive to how you measure them"

False friend: "sensible" (Spanish) = sensitive; "sensible" (English) = reasonable/practical.

- **Communicative impact:** high - changes meaning significantly; "sensible results" means reasonable results, not responsive ones
- **L1 transfer:** yes - "sensible" (Spanish) = sensitive; "sensato" (Spanish) = sensible (English)
- **Correction strategy:** explicit [10:15] - teacher explained both words clearly
- **Exercise priority:** 5

### #12 grammar/preposition (moderate) [16:25]

**Corrected:** "I was discussing this topic"

- **Communicative impact:** low - meaning clear
- **L1 transfer:** yes - "discutir sobre un tema" (Spanish) requires the preposition; English "discuss" takes a direct object
- **Correction strategy:** explicit [16:34] - teacher explained that "discuss" takes a direct object, offered alternatives "talk about" or "have a discussion about"
- **Exercise priority:** 3

### #16 grammar/conditional (moderate) [12:55]
